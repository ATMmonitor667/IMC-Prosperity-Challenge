ROUND 3 BOT IMPROVEMENT LOG
From 32,656 total PnL to 47,987 total PnL

1. Objective

The goal was not to make cosmetic changes to trader.py. The goal was to treat Round 3 as a quant research problem:

- Verify the exact API and backtest contract first.
- Inspect the local CSV structure.
- Measure product behavior directly from data.
- Form hypotheses about fair value, mean reversion, trend, ladder structure, and possible arbitrage.
- Implement stronger versions locally.
- Backtest each idea.
- Keep only the changes that improved PnL on the local round 3 days.

- ROUND3/trader.py

Key API findings from datamodel.py:

- The trader must implement Trader.run(self, state: TradingState).
- The return signature is:
  (Dict[str, List[Order]], int, str)
- The int is conversions.
- The str is traderData.

This mattered because I avoided making any assumptions about the return type.

- The VEV ladder behaves like a call option ladder on VELVET.
- Lower strikes have higher spot sensitivity.
- Higher strikes have lower spot sensitivity.


5.4 Surface / option-like analysis

I fit a simplified Black-Scholes style total-vol parameter to the VEV ladder using VELVET as the underlying reference.

Measured common total vol stats:

- mean about 0.0321
- median about 0.0323
- fairly tight range overall

I also checked convexity in strike across the ladder.

Result:

- zero convexity violations in the sample used

Main conclusion:

- The ladder is internally consistent and looks option-like.
- This justified testing surface-based fair values.


5.5 Residual / mean reversion tests

I tested residual signals like:

- option change minus beta * VELVET change
- option price minus fitted surface price

For the delta-adjusted residuals, I found:

- residual autocorrelation was negative
- residuals had predictive mean reversion for the next change

Examples:

- VEV_4000 residual next-change correlation about -0.336
- VEV_4500 about -0.287
- VEV_5300 about -0.267
- VEV_5400 about -0.301
- VEV_5500 about -0.262

Main conclusion:

- There was real cross-product structure in the data.
- That justified trying explicit surface / residual-based bots.

6. Hypotheses Formed From Data

The main working hypotheses were:

1. The round is primarily a short-horizon mean-reversion market making problem.
2. HYDROGEL should be handled independently.
3. VELVET should be traded cautiously because it can help but can also cause large giveback if overtraded.
4. VEV_5000 and VEV_5100 are the most connected to VELVET and therefore the easiest names to accidentally overtrade.
5. The ladder is option-like, but the simulator fill model may reward simple spread capture more than explicit theoretical arbitrage.


7. Strategy Experiments I Ran

7.1 Full surface / hedge prototype

I built a new trader that:

- estimated a common surface vol
- estimated option fair values from VELVET
- used option deltas
- tried hedge logic in VELVET

Result:

- Total PnL about -13,030

Why it failed:

- It over-rotated into explicit hedge behavior.
- The backtester fill model punished that style.
- VELVET losses dominated.


7.2 Hybrid surface + existing execution

I then kept more of the old execution logic and swapped in surface-based fair values for the VEV ladder.

Result:

- Total PnL about 4,697

Why it still failed:

- Better than the first rewrite, but still much worse than baseline.
- Explicit surface value was not translating into better realized fills in this simulator.


7.3 Ablation: remove VELVET entirely

I copied the baseline bot and disabled VELVET trading only.

Result:

- Total PnL about 33,300

Interpretation:

- This alone beat the original 32,656 baseline.
- That proved the current VELVET logic was too aggressive and hurting the portfolio.


7.4 Ablation: remove VELVET, VEV_5000, and VEV_5100

I then disabled the most correlated and unstable names together.

Result:

- Total PnL about 30,117

Interpretation:

- VEV_5000 and VEV_5100 were still net useful.
- The issue was not that they should be removed.
- The issue was that they needed tighter controls.


7.5 Tuned value-maker variant

I created a tuned version of the original architecture:

- more conservative VELVET taking
- more careful VEV_5000 and VEV_5100 thresholds
- same robust inventory-aware passive market making base

Result:

- Total PnL about 45,676

Interpretation:

- The profitable core was already in the original bot.
- The big win came from reducing bad aggression, not from replacing the architecture.


7.6 Parameter sweep and final convergence

I ran focused parameter sweeps around:

- VELVET min take edge
- VELVET take fraction
- VELVET make fraction
- drift threshold
- VEV_5000 / VEV_5100 take settings

Most important discovery from the sweep:

- Increasing DRIFT_T_THRESHOLD steadily improved results.
- At high enough values, the drift leg was effectively disabled.
- This was the strongest single improvement.

This confirmed the data intuition:

- Round 3 locally behaved much more like mean-reverting spread capture than trend following.


8. Final Changes That Produced 47,987

The final winning version kept the original architecture but changed the control parameters.

8.1 Drift leg disabled

In the final trader:

- DRIFT_T_THRESHOLD = 10.0

Effect:

- The strategy practically stops acting on weak trend estimates.
- It behaves like a pure value / spread market maker.

Why this helped:

- Trend signals were hurting out-of-sample local performance.
- Mean reversion was stronger and more reliable.


8.2 VELVET was made much more conservative

Final VELVET settings:

- k_take = 2.3
- min_take = 4
- min_make = 2
- take_frac = 0.14
- make_frac = 0.10
- vol_floor = 0.5

Why this helped:

- VELVET had enough edge to be worth trading, but only if the bot stopped forcing too many aggressive fills.
- Lower take size and wider take thresholds preserved profitable opportunities while removing a lot of noise trades.


8.3 VEV_5000 and VEV_5100 got special handling

Final settings for VEV_5000 and VEV_5100:

- k_take = 2.6
- k_make = 0.55
- min_take = 3
- min_make = 2
- take_frac = 0.20
- make_frac = 0.10
- vol_floor = 0.5

Why this helped:

- These were the most spot-linked VEV names.
- They needed tighter entry control than the generic VEV settings.
- The change preserved their upside while limiting bad inventory build-up.

10. Why the Final Version Worked Better

The improvement did not come from a fancy new theoretical model being pushed into production unchanged.

It came from three practical insights:

1. The original architecture was already structurally sound.
   It already had fair value estimation, market taking, passive quotes, inventory skew, limits, traderData state, and robust empty-book handling.

2. The main drag was bad aggressiveness, not missing complexity.
   The backtests showed that the trader was overtrading VELVET and some mid-ladder names.

3. In this simulator, cleaner spread capture beat explicit cross-product theory.
   The option-surface interpretation was real and useful for research, but direct surface-arb rewrites underperformed. The winning move was to use the research to tune where the existing execution logic was too loose.


11. Pain Points and Weaknesses

These are the main weaknesses that still remain and could be improved later.

11.1 No explicit live cross-product execution

Even though the VEV ladder clearly behaves like options on VELVET, the final bot still trades products mostly independently.

Potential future improvement:

- Build a residual-based cross-product executor that only acts when both legs are tradable at favorable prices.
- Do not use theoretical value alone; use executable spread-aware residual entry logic.


11.2 No deeper use of market trades / tape

The bot does not use market_trades aggressively for regime or flow inference.

Potential future improvement:

- Build simple signed tape pressure features.
- Distinguish between passive spread capture regimes and directional tape regimes.


11.3 No dynamic regime classification

The final bot uses online fair and vol updates, but it does not explicitly detect day-level or intraday regime changes.

Potential future improvement:

- Separate high-vol and low-vol states.
- Widen or narrow quoting automatically based on current realized volatility and fill quality.


11.4 No explicit fill-quality feedback loop

The bot assumes that a fixed take_frac / make_frac calibration remains good.

Potential future improvement:

- Track recent own fills from state.own_trades.
- If fills become too one-sided, widen or reduce size automatically.


11.5 VEV_6000 and VEV_6500 are basically ignored

They were close to static in the local sample, so they did not deserve aggressive attention.

Potential future improvement:

- Check if the official simulator or later unseen data allows edge from repeatedly quoting the wings.
- If fills at 0 or 1 are exploitable, a tiny dedicated wing logic may help.


11.6 Constants are still calibrated on only 3 local days

This is the most obvious overfitting risk.

Potential future improvement:

- Use rolling walk-forward validation if more hidden-like samples or alternative matching assumptions are available.
- Stress test under different trade matching settings in the backtester.


11.7 No observations-based conversion logic

There was no local evidence that conversions were supported in this Round 3 dataset.

Potential future improvement:

- If official round data includes observations/conversion inputs not present locally, add product-specific conversion arbitration immediately.


11.8 Fair value model is still simple

The final fair value is mostly online EWMA + vol-aware execution controls.

Potential future improvement:

- Add product-specific residual anchors or rolling z-scores.
- Use richer state in traderData for residual mean and residual vol, not only raw return statistics.


11.9 Backtester dependency risk

The final winner is the winner under the local backtester and its fill model.

Potential future improvement:

- Re-run under alternate match settings such as different trade matching assumptions.
- Compare sensitivity to fill assumptions before final submission.


12. Final Summary

The path from 32,656 to 47,987 was not about adding complexity for its own sake.

The process was:

- verify the exact API
- build a real local backtest loop
- measure the data
- test the option-surface hypothesis
- learn that explicit surface-arb underperformed
- ablate the weak products
- discover that VELVET aggression and drift-following were the main problems
- disable the harmful drift leg
- tighten VELVET and the most correlated VEV names
- confirm the final result with a clean backtest

Final practical lesson:

The best Round 3 bot in this local setup was a disciplined, inventory-aware, mean-reverting market maker with carefully tuned aggression, not a trend follower and not a fully explicit theoretical options arbitrage bot.


13. Addendum: Improvement From 47,987 to 61,305

After the first process log was written, I ran another iteration cycle using the same core lesson:

- keep the profitable market-making architecture
- avoid large theoretical rewrites
- improve only the parts that the data and backtests say are underexploited

13.1 New hypothesis

The first log already identified two remaining weaknesses:

- no residual-based inventory target
- HYDROGEL likely still under-optimized despite being one of the best books

The next idea was:

- let the spot-like books lean harder into mean reversion by using fair minus mid as an inventory target signal
- let HYDROGEL quote more passive size once its taking edge is made stricter

13.2 What was added

Two specific changes survived backtesting:

1. Residual-based inventory target on the spot-like books

Instead of always targeting flat inventory except for the disabled drift term, the bot now adds:

- a residual target based on (fair - mid) / take_edge

This is applied only to:

- VELVETFRUIT_EXTRACT
- HYDROGEL_PACK

Interpretation:

- if price is temporarily cheap versus the bot's fair estimate, the bot is willing to lean longer
- if price is temporarily rich, the bot is willing to lean shorter

This was the clean implementation of the "residual / rolling z-score" idea that had been identified earlier as a remaining opportunity.

2. Stronger HYDROGEL passive market making

The old HYDROGEL setup was still too conservative on passive quoting.

Final improved HYDROGEL settings:

- min_take = 8
- take_frac = 0.18
- make_frac = 0.19

Why it helped:

- HYDROGEL has a wide spread and strong standalone edge
- by demanding a stricter take edge, the bot avoids low-quality aggressive fills
- by allowing larger passive size, the bot captures much more of the spread when the book comes back

13.3 Final residual target scales

The best clean local result came from:

- VELVETFRUIT_EXTRACT residual scale = 1.05
- HYDROGEL_PACK residual scale = 0.45

Interpretation:

- VELVET benefited from much stronger directional leaning around its fair value
- HYDROGEL also benefited from leaning, but less aggressively than VELVET

13.4 Final verified result

Improved final result:

- Day 0: 22,760
- Day 1: 24,720
- Day 2: 13,825
- Total: 61,305

Improvement over the previous best logged version:

- 61,305 - 47,987 = 13,318

Improvement over the original 32,656 baseline:

- 61,305 - 32,656 = 28,649

That is roughly an 87.7 percent increase over the original baseline.

13.5 Updated practical lesson

The second iteration reinforced the same conclusion as the first:

- the edge in this local Round 3 setup comes from disciplined mean-reversion market making
- the best improvements come from better inventory targeting and better aggression control
- the biggest mistake is still overtrading weak edges

The new version is not better because it is more complicated everywhere.
It is better because it leans harder only where the local data consistently supports it:

- residual mean reversion in the spot-like books
- wide-spread spread capture in HYDROGEL
