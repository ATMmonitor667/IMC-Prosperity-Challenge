================================================================================
 SECTION 1 - ROUND 2 OVERVIEW ACROSS PROSPERITY EDITIONS
================================================================================

Round 2 in IMC Prosperity has historically been the round where the competition
moves beyond "one stable product + one noisy product" market making and forces
you to reason about relationships between products (or between a product and
an external exchange / observable factors). Exact products change each year;
the underlying concepts repeat.

--- Prosperity 1 (2023) -----------------------------------------------------
  Products: COCONUTS, PINA_COLADAS
  Core idea: highly correlated pair (>90%), classic PAIR TRADING.
  Canonical solution: compute spread = P(PINA_COLADAS) - P(COCONUTS), take
  z-score, short the spread when z > 1.5, long when z < -1.5, flatten
  near zero. Hedge ratio sometimes refined with OLS (example: 1.551 slope
  seen in public writeups).

--- Prosperity 2 (2024) -----------------------------------------------------
  Product: ORCHIDS (plus continuing AMETHYSTS / STARFRUIT from R1).
  Twist: ORCHIDS could be traded both on the main island and on the South
  Archipelago via a CONVERSION primitive, with import/export tariffs,
  transport fees, and observable factors (sunlight, humidity, storage).
  Canonical solution: CROSS-EXCHANGE ARBITRAGE rather than directional
  prediction. Maximize (enter price here - exit price abroad - costs)
  * execution probability. Teams that tried to regress ORCHIDS on sunlight
  or humidity generally lost to teams that just arb'd the two venues.

--- Prosperity 3 (2025) -----------------------------------------------------
  Products: CROISSANTS, JAMS, DJEMBES, PICNIC_BASKET1, PICNIC_BASKET2.
  PB1 = 6 CROISSANTS + 3 JAMS + 1 DJEMBE
  PB2 = 4 CROISSANTS + 2 JAMS
  Core idea: ETF-STYLE STATISTICAL ARBITRAGE. Compare basket price to the
  sum of weighted component prices (synthetic). Trade the spread using
  z-score thresholds. Top teams also did "two-way" pricing: use the
  basket to imply a component fair and vice versa.

--- Prosperity 4 (this workspace) -------------------------------------------
  Products observed in the CSVs:
    - ASH_COATED_OSMIUM
    - INTARIAN_PEPPER_ROOT
  See: ROUND2/prices_round_2_day_-1.csv, day_0.csv, day_1.csv
  Observed behavior (from offline analysis and tuned traders):
    - Osmium: mid pinned near 10,001 with tiny std, strong tick-level mean
      reversion (lag-1 return autocorrelation ~ -0.50).
    - Pepper: drifts +~1000 across a simulated day (day -1: 11k -> 12k;
      day 0: 12k -> 13k; day 1: 13k -> 14k). Locally the detrended std
      is small (~2.2) and also shows tick-level mean reversion.
  Paired relationship:
    - Global correlation of the two mids is weak (~ -0.08).
    - Rolling correlation swings in [-0.83, +0.69].
    - OLS residuals are NOT cleanly stationary (ADF p ~ 0.77).
  Implication: no static pairs trade is safe; independent market making
  per product is the dependable edge, pair/spread logic is an OPTIONAL
  OVERLAY that must be gated by online correlation stability.


================================================================================
 SECTION 2 - CORE TRADING CONCEPTS TO KNOW FOR ROUND 2
================================================================================

2.1 MARKET MICROSTRUCTURE
    - Mid price: (best_bid + best_ask) / 2. Simple, but noisy when one side
      has thin volume.
    - Microprice: volume-weighted mid:
          microprice = (best_bid * ask_volume + best_ask * bid_volume)
                       / (bid_volume + ask_volume)
      Slightly better short-term fair because it leans toward the heavier
      side (pressure indicator).
    - L3 order book: bid_price_1..3 / ask_price_1..3 with volumes. Depth
      beyond the top is useful for imbalance and for gauging how much size
      you can absorb before the book "moves."
    - Quoted spread: ask1 - bid1. Determines how wide you must quote to
      avoid immediate adverse selection.
    - Imbalance: (sum_bid_vol - sum_ask_vol) / (sum_bid_vol + sum_ask_vol).
      Short-term pressure signal, capped so it never dominates fair.

2.2 FAIR VALUE MODELS
    - Fixed anchor: for strongly stable products (think Pearls / Amethysts
      / Rainforest Resin) fair = constant from offline mean.
    - EMA of mid or microprice:
          ema_t = alpha * x_t + (1 - alpha) * ema_{t-1}
      Small alpha -> smooth slow fair; larger alpha -> reactive. Pandas
      "span" convention: alpha = 2 / (N + 1).
    - Fast/slow EMA + slope: fast EMA tracks level, slow EMA tracks trend,
      slope = (ema - ema_prev) / dt. fair = ema + slope * forecast_horizon.
    - Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (mean-reverting) model: dS = theta * (mu - S) dt
      + sigma dW. Used by rank-13 team pe049395 in Prosperity 2 to price
      Amethysts-style products and to set quote sizes from execution
      probability.
    - Linear model for composites (basket): fair_basket = a + b1 * P1
      + b2 * P2 + ... where (a, b_i) are fit by OLS on historical data.
      CarterT27 (P3, 9th global) used this instead of nominal recipe.

2.3 STATISTICAL ARBITRAGE
    - Correlation vs cointegration: correlation says returns co-move, but
      prices can still drift apart forever. Cointegration says a linear
      combination of the (non-stationary) prices is stationary, which is
      what you actually need for a mean-reverting spread.
    - Pair trading recipe:
        1. Estimate hedge ratio b (OLS: y = a + b * x).
        2. Form spread = y - b * x.
        3. Standardize: z = (spread - mean) / std (rolling or anchored).
        4. Short spread when z > +thr, long when z < -thr, flatten near 0.
    - Half-life of mean reversion (from AR(1) on residuals):
          phi = Corr(resid_t, resid_{t-1})
          half_life = -ln(2) / ln(phi)     (ticks)
      Long half-life -> do not expect a quick round trip; scale position
      and horizon accordingly.
    - ADF test (stationarity of residuals): low p-value = evidence of
      cointegration. Beware small samples / regime shifts.

2.4 ETF / BASKET ARBITRAGE
    - Synthetic basket: sum of component prices with known (or fitted)
      weights.
    - Two pricing directions:
        basket_fair = synthetic
        component_fair = basket_price / recipe (or via constraints
                          when multiple baskets share components)
    - Practical choices:
        * Trade only the basket against synthetic to minimize slippage
          (pe049395 P2).
        * Trade both sides for a market-neutral hedge (hochfilzer P2,
          Linear Utility P2 / ericcccsliu, Oravec P3).
    - Spread signal again: z-score with mean anchored to fundamentals
      (basket premium) and std from a small rolling window (Linear
      Utility's "modified z-score"). Small window means z spikes when
      vol compresses - often right before reversion.

2.5 CONVERSIONS AND CROSS-EXCHANGE ARBITRAGE
    (Relevant if a Prosperity 4 round exposes a South-Archipelago-style
    conversion. Not in the current Round 2 data but listed for
    completeness.)
    - Per-timestamp conversion at foreign bid/ask after tariffs + fees.
    - Arb only trades that net positive after all costs.
    - Execution probability matters: posting "at fair" does not fill well
      when there is a fixed foreign price; pe049395 used Poisson
      probability modeling instead of exponential.

2.6 INVENTORY MANAGEMENT
    - Hard position limit per product (the simulator enforces).
    - Skew: shift both quotes by a function of current position so you
      lean back toward zero (e.g., skew = pos // SKEW_DENOM).
    - Soft cap: above some |pos| threshold, shrink the side that would
      grow the position further.
    - Target bias: for a drifting product, inventory target != 0 can be
      optimal (e.g., long bias on a name that drifts up). Gate this on
      the signal that produced the bias so you flatten when it fades.

2.7 EXECUTION
    - Market-taking (crossing the book): pays the spread, guaranteed
      fill, used when book price is already past your fair by more than
      a vol-scaled threshold.
    - Passive quoting (resting orders): earns spread, uncertain fill,
      exposed to adverse selection.
    - Layering: near layer close to fair (high fill rate, small size),
      far layer further out (lower fill rate, catches spread widenings).
    - Slippage: your backtest fill model is not reality - always expect
      actual PnL to be below backtest PnL. Linear Utility explicitly
      reported losing ~20k seashells to slippage vs backtest in P2 R3.

2.8 TREND VS MEAN REVERSION
    - Diagnostic: lag-1 autocorrelation of mid returns.
        * AC1 < 0: tick-level mean reversion (MM-friendly).
        * AC1 > 0: momentum (trend-following friendly).
        * AC1 ~ 0: random walk / efficient at that horizon.
    - A product can be MEAN-REVERTING at tick scale AND TRENDING at day
      scale (the pepper case). You need BOTH:
        * local MR for MM edges and inventory skew
        * a slow trend lean so you do not systematically fade the drift

2.9 RISK AND META-RISKS
    - Overfitting: 3 historical days is little data. Simpler > fancier.
    - Regime change: live day may have different vol/drift than train.
    - Day-boundary artifacts: concatenating days produces fake jumps
      unless you include `day` in the index.
    - Overcomplicated logic: 90% of alpha in Prosperity comes from
      correctly identifying fair and managing inventory; the rest is
      parameter tuning.

 SECTION 3 - DATA ANALYSIS PLAYBOOK FOR ROUND 2 CSVs
================================================================================

Files you have:
  prices_round_2_day_{-1, 0, 1}.csv
    Schema: day;timestamp;product;bid_price_1;bid_volume_1;...;ask_price_3;
            ask_volume_3;mid_price;profit_and_loss
  trades_round_2_day_{-1, 0, 1}.csv
    Schema: timestamp;buyer;seller;symbol;currency;price;quantity

Run everything in pandas. The checklist below is ordered from cheapest /
most revealing to most expensive / most speculative.

3.1 DATA HYGIENE (do this before any stat)
    - Drop rows with mid_price == 0 (empty book). Keep a counter so you
      know how many ticks you discarded per product per day.
    - Sort by (day, timestamp). Reset index.
    - Check for duplicates on (day, timestamp, product) - there should be
      at most one row each; duplicates usually indicate parsing issues.
    - Compute ACTUAL tick spacing: `df.groupby("day")["timestamp"].diff()
      .describe()` - should mostly be 100.
    - Always analyze PER (product, day) first, then pool across days only
      after you have seen per-day numbers.

3.2 UNIVARIATE (PER PRODUCT, PER DAY)
    - Mid level: mean, median, std, min, max.
    - Returns: r_t = mid_t - mid_{t-1}. Report std, skew, extremes, and
      the fraction of |r_t| > 3 ticks.
    - Lag-1 autocorrelation of returns: the single most informative stat
      for whether to MM or trend-follow.
    - Detrended std: subtract a rolling mean (e.g. 200-tick) or a linear
      time trend, then std. For pepper-like products this is MUCH smaller
      than raw std and tells the real tick-noise story.
    - Rolling std of returns (window 100-500): shows vol regimes.

3.3 MICROSTRUCTURE
    - Quoted spread (ask1 - bid1) distribution: mean, median, p10, p90.
      Plot a histogram - a bimodal shape (tight + wide) means two
      regimes, and you may want conditional quoting.
    - Depth at L1, L1+L2, L1+L2+L3 (sum of volumes). Mean and by-day.
    - Microprice - mid distribution: shows how often the book is heavily
      one-sided.
    - Imbalance distribution.

3.4 TREND VS NOISE
    - Fit mid ~ a + b * timestamp per (product, day). Report slope b
      (ticks per timestamp) and residual std. Large slope + small resid
      std = steady drift (e.g. pepper).
    - Plot residuals - they should look stationary if your detrending
      is adequate.

3.5 CROSS-ASSET (OSMIUM vs PEPPER, or any pair / basket you have)
    - Pivot to wide form on (day, timestamp), keep only rows where both
      mids > 0.
    - Pooled correlation AND per-day correlation. Sign flips between
      days are a red flag for any fixed pair trade.
    - Rolling correlation (window 500-2000 ticks). Report min/max/mean
      of the rolling series.
    - OLS hedge ratio: y = a + b * x. Report b, a, residual std.
    - AR(1) on residuals: phi and half-life.
    - ADF on residuals (statsmodels.tsa.stattools.adfuller): watch
      p-value but do not treat it as decisive on 3 days of data.
    - Lead-lag: cross-correlation of RETURNS at lags -5..+5. Rarely
      strong in Prosperity, but worth a check.

3.6 BASKET-STYLE ANALYSIS (applicable when a basket exists)
    - Build synthetic = sum of w_i * P_i using either the nominal recipe
      or OLS-fitted weights.
    - spread = basket - synthetic. Plot, histogram, rolling mean, rolling
      std.
    - z-score with (mean, std) computed online using a rolling window.
      Try both small (50-200) and large (1000-2000) windows and see
      which produces cleaner signals.
    - Compare two-way: also price components from basket and check
      residual structure.

3.7 TRADES FILE
    - Volume per hour (or per 10k timestamps) per product.
    - Distribution of trade size.
    - Merge on (timestamp, symbol) with prices to get trade_price minus
      contemporaneous mid. Most trades should be inside the spread;
      significant "through the spread" volume indicates informed flow.
    - Imbalance of buyer vs seller (if the CSV exposes counterparties).

3.8 VISUALIZATION PATTERNS THAT PAY OFF
    - Mid vs time, one panel per day. Catches spikes, zero-mid ticks,
      day-boundary drift.
    - Histogram of quoted spread per product.
    - Scatter of product_A mid vs product_B mid, colored by day.
    - Rolling correlation series overlaid on the two mids.
    - For basket style: spread vs time with +/- threshold bands.

3.9 WALK-FORWARD DISCIPLINE
    - Use day -1 and day 0 for discovery / tuning.
    - Hold out day 1 as an out-of-sample check.
    - Rotate holdouts occasionally to see which day is actually
      representative.


================================================================================
 SECTION 4 - TECHNIQUES DISTILLED FROM PAST WINNERS
================================================================================

Each entry lists the team, what they did, why it worked, and how to adapt
it to any Round 2 setup (pair, basket, or cross-exchange).

4.1 Two-way synthetic pricing
    Sources: Martin Oravec (P3), TimoDiehm (P3, 2nd global)
    Idea: do not only price basket = f(components); also price each
    component = g(basket, other_components). Both sides cross-check and
    produce more trading opportunities.
    Adapt: when you have any composite asset, write two fair functions
    and reconcile them. Trade whichever leg has the larger z.

4.2 Modified z-score with small rolling window
    Source: Linear Utility / ericcccsliu (P2, 2nd global)
    Idea: anchor the mean to a fundamental (historical average basket
    premium) but compute std on a SMALL rolling window. When vol
    compresses, z spikes even at modest deviations, catching reversion
    near local extrema.
    Adapt: in any spread strategy, try fixed-mean-rolling-std alongside
    the classic rolling-mean-rolling-std z-score.

4.3 Scaled position sizing via a continuous score
    Source: Johnny Tung (top 2.5% P3)
    Idea: rather than binary "short when z > 1.5", compute a continuous
    long_index and scale position linearly up to the position limit.
    Smoother PnL, fewer whipsaws near thresholds.
    Adapt: your inventory TARGET can be a smooth function of spread /
    slope / imbalance.

4.4 OLS-fitted weights instead of nominal recipe
    Source: CarterT27 (9th global, P3)
    Idea: even if the basket is specified as "6 A + 3 B + 1 C", market
    behavior may follow a slightly different effective weighting. Fit
    w by OLS on historical data; spread using fitted w.
    Adapt: always compare "nominal synthetic" and "OLS synthetic"
    residual std; use whichever is smaller (and stationary).

4.5 Monte Carlo / data augmentation for parameter tuning
    Source: pe049395 (Rank 13, P2)
    Idea: simulate many perturbed histories from the observed data to
    avoid overfitting thresholds to one or two days.
    Adapt: if tuning z-thresholds, resample / bootstrap the time series
    and tune to robust medians instead of one run's max.

4.6 Hardcoded fundamental mean + local std
    Source: Linear Utility
    Reasoning given: "there should be a fundamental reason behind the
    mean of spread; the volatility each day is less predictable." So
    separate the two.
    Adapt: if you know the long-run fair (e.g. Osmium ~ 10,001), DO
    hardcode it; then estimate only vol online.

4.7 Trade timing near local extrema
    Source: Linear Utility
    Idea: hitting +/- z thresholds often happens just before reversion;
    backtest where on the distribution your fills land and place
    thresholds so you are closer to extrema even at the cost of fewer
    trades.
    Adapt: report fill histograms in your backtest, not just total PnL.

4.8 Correlation gate for pairs overlay
    Source: our own Round 2 work
    Idea: enable pairs/spread overlay only when online rolling correlation
    of returns exceeds a threshold; damp it when correlation is weak or
    flipping. Prevents the overlay from destroying PnL in uncorrelated
    regimes.
    Adapt: always gate optional overlays on a live stability test.

4.9 Trend-aware market making
    Source: our own pepper strategy (and implied in many P2 writeups)
    Idea: for a drifting product, compute slope, shift fair by
    slope * forecast_horizon, and set inventory target biased in the
    trend direction. Keep tight quotes on the trend-friendly side and
    wider quotes on the fighting side.
    Adapt: use fast/slow EMA and an EWMA of the slope, not a raw diff.

4.10 Ornstein-Uhlenbeck MM with execution probability
    Source: pe049395 (P2)
    Idea: model dS = theta (mu - S) dt + sigma dW; size quotes by
    expected utility = (edge + inventory_risk_adjustment) * P(fill),
    where P(fill) is a Poisson function of quote distance.
    Adapt: overkill for most teams, but the "expected utility per quote"
    framework is worth internalizing.

 SECTION 6 - PITFALLS TO AVOID
================================================================================

6.1 Overfitting to 3 days
    - If a threshold improves day 0 PnL by 5k but hurts day 1 by 7k,
      you are overfitting. Prefer configurations that are monotone
      across days.

6.2 Fixed hedge ratios without stationarity evidence
    - A "pair" can have b = -14 or +1 across different windows here.
    - Always report AR(1) phi and ADF p on residuals BEFORE trading a
      spread, and be willing to skip the overlay if they are bad.

6.3 Look-ahead bias
    - Any feature you compute in a notebook must only use data <= t
      at time t if you plan to implement it live. Rolling windows
      without `closed="left"` are a classic mistake.

6.4 Drift vs volatility confusion
    - Pepper's full-day std (~290) is DRIFT, not tick noise. Use
      detrended std (~2.2) when sizing edges and inventory.

6.5 Stitching days without day boundaries
    - Concatenating CSVs and computing returns across the join creates
      a fake massive move. Keep `day` in the index; compute stats per
      day then aggregate.

6.6 Chasing the last signal
    - Each round only has a few days of data. Sophisticated models
      rarely beat disciplined MM + inventory control. The second-place
      Prosperity 2 team literally said "the only thing that mattered
      for profitability here was the distance between the trade price
      and the true fair." Keep that in mind before adding complexity.

6.7 Trusting a single number
    - Pooled correlation, pooled ADF, pooled std - all can lie when
      regimes switch. Always break out per-day.

6.8 Backtest != live
    - Expect ~10-20% PnL haircut in live competition vs your backtest.
      Do not submit a config that only just barely wins locally.
